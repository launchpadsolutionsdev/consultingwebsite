Regulation is the single most constraining variable on charitable raffle programs. A platform, prize structure, and supporter list can all be fixed. Jurisdictional rules cannot — they define the outer boundary of what's possible in any given market.

This report provides an overview of where online and electronic raffles stand across North America, as of Q1 2026, and how charity leadership should think about regulatory planning for 2026–2028.

<Callout type="warning">
  This report is a strategic overview, not legal advice. Licensing rules change; local interpretation varies; the penalty for getting this wrong is severe. For binding guidance on your specific program, consult a lawyer experienced in charitable gaming law — or an AGCO-registered (or equivalent state-registered) raffle management partner.
</Callout>

## Canada: the mature market

### Ontario
The AGCO (Alcohol and Gaming Commission of Ontario) operates the most mature and most-copied charitable gaming framework in North America.

**Key points:**
- Raffles **up to $50,000** in total prizes: **municipal** licensing.
- Raffles **above $50,000** or conducted via the internet: **AGCO provincial** licensing.
- Catch the Ace is explicitly permitted under a progressive raffle framework with detailed operating controls.
- Electronic raffle suppliers must be AGCO-registered as Gaming-Related Suppliers.

Ontario is the benchmark. Most other provinces and many U.S. states look to the AGCO framework when modernizing their own.

### British Columbia
Licensed through the **Gaming Policy and Enforcement Branch (GPEB)**. Electronic raffles are permitted; Class A, B, C, and D gaming licenses cover different prize thresholds and event types. Catch the Ace is licensed on a case-by-case basis.

### Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba (Prairie provinces)
Licensed through provincial gaming commissions (AGLC in Alberta, SLGA in Saskatchewan, Liquor Gaming & Cannabis Authority of Manitoba). Electronic raffles are permitted with licensing; the frameworks are less uniform than Ontario's, with more variation in how progressive raffles are treated.

### Quebec
Licensed through **Régie des alcools, des courses et des jeux**. Electronic raffles are permitted; bilingual requirements and specific French-language compliance apply to all marketing and communications.

### Atlantic provinces (NS, NB, NL, PEI)
Historically smaller markets but increasingly active in Catch the Ace specifically. Each province maintains its own framework; Nova Scotia and Newfoundland have seen rapid adoption.

## United States: the patchwork

U.S. raffle law is fundamentally state-level, with enormous variation. Below is a simplified current-state overview; the legislative picture is changing quickly and this should be considered a snapshot, not a permanent reference.

### States that permit online raffle ticket sales (14 as of Q1 2026)
Colorado, Georgia, Illinois, Indiana, Kentucky, Maine, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New Jersey, North Dakota, Pennsylvania, Virginia, Wisconsin.

Frameworks, thresholds, and fee structures vary significantly. Georgia and Pennsylvania are among the more active markets; Illinois and Michigan have mature programs tied to major pro sports foundations.

### States with active legislation (as of Q1 2026)
Arizona, Florida, Massachusetts, Ohio, Texas — all have legislation either introduced, advancing, or in pilot. If any or all pass, the number of permitting states could rise to 19 by end of 2026.

### States permitting in-person raffles only
Most remaining states. Charities can run in-person raffle programs (typically at events or tied to specific live gatherings) but cannot legally sell tickets online to residents.

### States prohibiting paid-entry raffles entirely
Alabama, Hawaii, Utah. Free-entry sweepstakes with alternative methods of entry (AMOE) may still be permissible but require specialized legal guidance.

## The cross-border problem

This is where most programs get into trouble.

A Canadian charity running an AGCO-licensed 50/50 cannot, by default, sell tickets to U.S. residents — even if the cause is explicitly cross-border. Doing so exposes the charity to U.S. gambling-law jurisdiction and can invalidate the AGCO license.

Similarly, a U.S. charity in a state that permits online raffles cannot sell to residents of a state that prohibits them. Most compliant platforms geo-block at the IP and address-verification level.

**Strategic implication:** Before designing a cross-border program, the licensing picture must be mapped jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction. Some combinations work cleanly (Ontario + cross-border states where a separate U.S. charitable entity holds a U.S. license). Many combinations don't.

<PullQuote>
  The single most common regulatory mistake we see is charities assuming that because their cause is national, their raffle can be too. It can't — at least not without structuring a program that respects each jurisdiction's licensing requirements independently.
</PullQuote>

## Supplier registration

In most mature jurisdictions, **raffle platforms themselves** must be registered with the regulator as gaming-related suppliers. In Ontario, this is the AGCO's Gaming-Related Supplier registration. In British Columbia, the GPEB. In Michigan, a specific Michigan Gaming Control Board registration.

**Charities frequently don't know this.** A platform may be an excellent technology but unable to legally operate in a given jurisdiction. Before signing a platform contract, confirm:

1. The platform is currently registered in every jurisdiction you intend to operate in.
2. The registration is active, not pending or expired.
3. Any registration restrictions on scope (some platforms are registered for certain formats only).

## Licensing timelines by jurisdiction

For charity leadership planning launches, realistic expected timelines:

| Jurisdiction | First-time applicant | Returning applicant |
|---|---|---|
| Ontario (AGCO) | 12–18 weeks | 4–8 weeks |
| British Columbia (GPEB) | 8–14 weeks | 4–6 weeks |
| Michigan | 10–16 weeks | 6–10 weeks |
| Pennsylvania | 8–14 weeks | 4–8 weeks |
| Most U.S. states | 10–20 weeks | 6–12 weeks |

**Takeaway:** Any program aiming for a specific launch date (anniversary, season opener, event) should begin licensing work **at least 6 months in advance**. Programs that try to compress this routinely miss.

## What's changing in 2026

Several regulatory trends are worth watching:

**1. U.S. state legalization.** The trajectory is clearly toward more online raffle permissions. Expect 2–4 more states to pass enabling legislation in 2026 alone.

**2. Platform registration harmonization.** Regulators are increasingly sharing information. A platform with clean registration in Ontario, Michigan, and Pennsylvania has an easier path to new jurisdictions than one with gaps.

**3. Progressive raffle frameworks.** Catch the Ace specifically is getting its own formal frameworks in jurisdictions that previously treated it under general raffle rules. This is generally good for operators — clearer rules, fewer gray areas.

**4. Consumer-protection scrutiny.** Expect more focus on responsible gaming, ticket-buyer identification, and spend-limit compliance. Programs that have already built responsible gaming infrastructure will be ahead.

**5. Cross-border clarification.** Expect formal guidance (or at least enforcement pattern) on cross-border programs. The current gray zone is unsustainable as the industry grows.

## How to use this report

This report is a planning tool, not a compliance document. If you're considering a raffle program:

1. Identify the specific jurisdictions where your charity is registered and where your supporter base lives.
2. Map the regulatory landscape for those jurisdictions using this report as a starting point.
3. Confirm platform registration in each target jurisdiction before signing any contracts.
4. Build a 6+ month runway into your launch timeline for first-time licensing.
5. If you're operating across multiple jurisdictions, work with a partner who has current active registrations in each.
