When industry analysts project online charitable raffles to grow at **11.2% compound annual growth rate through 2030** — outpacing broader iGaming categories at roughly 6% — it's worth asking: *why?* iGaming is a massively capitalized, mature industry with best-in-class marketing, technology, and regulatory relationships. Charitable raffles are, in comparison, a quaint cottage industry. How is the smaller, sleepier market growing twice as fast?

The answer reveals something important about where philanthropic engagement is heading — and why the smart money in charity fundraising is flowing toward this category.

Three categories. Three very different trajectories.

## Why online raffles are accelerating

### 1. They're coming off a much smaller base

Part of the story is pure math. Online raffles were a rounding error on the broader gaming industry as recently as 2018. Every percentage point of new market adoption translates to outsized growth on a smaller denominator. This effect will naturally taper as the category matures, but it has years of runway left.

### 2. Regulatory expansion is still unlocking markets

iGaming has largely fought its regulatory battles. Online sports betting in the U.S., for example, expanded dramatically between 2018–2024 and is now approaching the limit of states that will permit it.

Online charitable raffles are in an earlier stage of that curve. 14 U.S. states currently permit them; another 5 have active legislation. Over the next five years, we expect the permitted state count to approximately double. Every new state adds a fresh tranche of addressable market — and charities in those states typically launch programs within 12–18 months.

### 3. The cause premium

This is the most underrated structural factor.

In iGaming, the operator is capturing the margin. Supporters know this. There's a subtle friction in the transaction — a lingering awareness that "the house wins in the long run."

In a charitable raffle, that friction evaporates. The "house" is the cause — the hospital, the rotary club, the junior hockey team. Supporters feel good about spending, regardless of whether they win. That emotional re-framing produces:

- Higher per-capita spend.
- Higher repeat-purchase rates.
- Dramatically lower customer acquisition cost (because social sharing and word-of-mouth work harder for cause-driven purchases).

  A $50 iGaming bet feels like a $50 bet. A $50 raffle ticket feels like a $50 contribution to the cause, with an unexpected upside. The math is identical; the psychology is entirely different \u2014 and the psychology drives economics.

### 4. Lower CAC, higher LTV

We've analyzed customer acquisition cost (CAC) and lifetime value (LTV) patterns across our client portfolio and compared against publicly disclosed iGaming benchmarks. The pattern is striking:

| Metric | Charitable raffle | Online gambling |
|---|---|---|
| Average CAC (first-ticket) | $8–$18 | $150–$400+ |
| First-year LTV | $60–$140 | $200–$800 |
| LTV:CAC ratio | 5–10x | 1.5–3x |
| Organic / referral share | 40–60% | 5–15% |

Raffles don't require the same level of paid acquisition because the cause does the selling. That's a structural economic advantage that compounds over time.

### 5. Mobile-native user experience

Most charitable raffle platforms today are designed mobile-first, with Apple Pay / Google Pay checkouts, one-tap ticket purchases, and responsive landing pages. The user experience is competitive with consumer e-commerce.

This hasn't always been true. Ten years ago, most raffle platforms were clunky desktop affairs. The modernization of the platform layer over the past 5–7 years is a significant driver of the acceleration.

### 6. Demographics are finally aligning

Baby boomers — a historically generous cohort — are aging out of direct-mail responsiveness but are comfortable with online transactions for causes they care about. Millennials and Gen X — comfortable with digital transactions and tapped-out on traditional fundraising asks — respond well to raffle mechanics because they feel participatory rather than extractive.

The demographic alignment works in favor of charitable raffles in a way it doesn't fully work for direct mail, galas, or major-gift pipelines.

## Where the growth won't come from

It's important to note where growth *isn't* coming from:

**Not from stealing from other fundraising channels.** Early fears that raffles would cannibalize donor giving have largely not materialized. Well-run raffle programs have net-neutral or slightly positive effects on other giving — the raffle ticket-buyer is often a different persona than the monthly donor, and the two rarely trade against each other.

**Not from aggressive marketing tactics.** The growth curve is not being fueled by dark-pattern dunks. It's being fueled by the structural factors above, plus steady execution improvement at the program level.

**Not from a single "killer" platform.** No single technology is creating the growth. It's distributed across the platform layer, with BUMP, Stride, Rosewood, and emerging entrants each contributing.

## What this means for charities

If you're a charity leader trying to think three years ahead, the implications are fairly clear:

**1. The opportunity window is wide open right now.** Markets growing at 11% annually reward early, well-executed entrants disproportionately. The charities building programs in 2024–2026 will have a structural advantage over those starting in 2028.

**2. Cross-category comparisons favor investment.** If you're weighing whether to build capability in online raffles vs. digital direct response vs. planned giving, the growth rate of online raffles is meaningfully higher than any other comparable channel.

**3. The CAC advantage compounds.** Every year a charity invests in its raffle program is a year it's acquiring low-cost ticket buyers who become repeat supporters. Delay has a measurable cost.

**4. Operational capability is the moat.** Since the platforms are commoditizing and the growth is structural, the differentiation between charities will be operational — which means the investment in operational capability (in-house or through a partner) is where the leverage is.

## What this means for investors and platforms

The platform layer is consolidating, and it will continue. The economics of running a full-stack charitable gaming platform — regulatory relationships across multiple jurisdictions, customer support at scale, payment processing, anti-fraud — reward scale. Expect continued M&A.

We also expect adjacent categories (AI-driven marketing tooling, payment processing, compliance-as-a-service) to see new entrants specifically targeting the charitable gaming vertical.

## The takeaway

11.2% CAGR isn't a fluke. It's the output of six structural factors — small base effects, regulatory expansion, cause-driven psychology, favorable unit economics, mobile-native UX, and demographic alignment — all pushing in the same direction.

Growth rates like that are rare, and the window won't stay open forever. Charity leadership that takes the data seriously will be positioned for the next decade of philanthropic engagement.
