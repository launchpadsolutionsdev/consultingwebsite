Charitable gaming in North America is quietly having its moment. For decades a fragmented patchwork of pull-tab sales, church bingo nights, and hockey-game 50/50s, the industry has in the past five years become something meaningfully different: a digital-first, high-growth vertical within nonprofit fundraising that increasingly resembles iGaming in its mechanics while remaining firmly rooted in community philanthropy.

This report synthesizes what we're seeing across our client base, publicly available industry data, and regulator publications. It's intended as a strategic-level overview for charity leadership and boards planning raffle programs over the next 12–36 months.

## The shape of the market

Charitable gaming in North America is composed of five major subcategories:

1. **Electronic raffles** — 50/50, Catch the Ace, progressive prize raffles (the fastest-growing segment)
2. **Pull-tabs** — paper and, increasingly, electronic pull-tab terminals
3. **Bingo** — paper, electronic terminals, and increasingly online bingo
4. **Charity poker & tournaments** — limited geographic footprint
5. **Lotteries** — larger provincial and state-sanctioned programs with charitable allocations

For the purposes of this report, we focus on electronic raffles — the segment where growth is highest, technology is evolving fastest, and charity-to-charity performance variance is widest.

## Growth: the 11.2% story

Industry analysts project the online raffle segment to grow at a compound annual rate of **11.2% through 2030**. To put that in context:

That growth isn't uniform. It's heavily concentrated in:

- **Well-run hospital foundations** in mid-sized cities with strong community identity.
- **Pro sports team foundations** (CFL, NHL, MLS, NBA, college athletics).
- **Regional rotary and service clubs** with dedicated volunteer labor.
- **Service-sector charities** (first responders, veterans, search & rescue) with high-affinity supporter bases.

Charities without those structural advantages grow more slowly — but grow they do. The rising tide has lifted nearly every well-operated program.

## Where the growth is coming from

### 1. Mobile-first ticket sales

Five years ago, most charitable raffle sales were in-person — at games, at galas, at the church basement. Today, for the programs we work with, mobile drives **70–90% of ticket volume**. Desktop is second; in-person tablet sales (POS at live events) is a meaningful third.

The implication: every program needs a checkout flow optimized for a 5-inch screen, Apple Pay / Google Pay at the bare minimum, and an SMS confirmation that works on any carrier.

### 2. Recurring subscriptions

Several platforms — notably Stride and BUMP CBN — now support recurring ticket subscriptions. A supporter can enroll to automatically purchase tickets every week or every draw, until they cancel.

For programs that have added subscriptions thoughtfully, the impact is significant: **15–30% of cycle revenue** attributable to subscription purchases, with much lower CAC than acquiring a fresh buyer every cycle.

### 3. SMS as a revenue channel

SMS has gone from novelty to essential. Charities that have added compliant, consent-based SMS campaigns are seeing **10–20% lift** in final-week revenue, at near-zero marginal cost per send. The open rates dwarf email.

### 4. Progressive format adoption

Catch the Ace was barely known outside Ontario in 2019. By 2026, it's spreading through Atlantic Canada, parts of the Prairies, and pilot programs in several U.S. states. The viral mechanics of a rolling jackpot have proven transferable.

## Regulatory landscape

Regulation remains the most significant operational constraint on charitable raffles.

**Canada** is relatively permissive. Provincial frameworks exist in every province, though with meaningfully different thresholds, reporting requirements, and electronic-raffle permissions. Ontario (AGCO) is the most mature and most-copied framework.

**The United States** is a patchwork:
- 14 states currently permit online raffle ticket sales.
- 5 more have pending legislation.
- Alabama, Hawaii, and Utah prohibit paid-entry raffles entirely.
- Many states permit in-person raffles but prohibit online sales — a structural constraint that severely caps program growth.

## Platform landscape

The platform layer is consolidating, and fast. A decade ago, a charity had a handful of regional options. Today, the market is structured around four clear tiers:

### Tier 1: Full-stack raffle platforms (BUMP CBN, Stride)
Enterprise-grade platforms with their own regulatory registrations, dedicated charity teams, and track records of running 8–10 figure programs. Pricing is higher; capability is correspondingly higher.

### Tier 2: Multi-purpose fundraising platforms (Funraise, GiveSmart, Zeffy)
Platforms that support raffles as one of many fundraising products. Broader feature set, thinner raffle-specific depth, lower pricing — suitable for smaller programs.

### Tier 3: Specialized regional operators
Rosewood (Ontario, Catch the Ace), smaller operators with geographic specialization. Often well-integrated with regional regulatory bodies.

### Tier 4: DIY / adjacent tools
General-purpose event platforms, form builders, custom development. Rarely the right answer for serious programs but used by some very small charities.

<PullQuote>
  Platform selection is less consequential than most charity boards believe. The performance gap within a tier is typically 20–40%. The performance gap across operational capability — regardless of platform — is often 3–5x.
</PullQuote>

## The performance gap: operational capability widens the winner-loser spread

One of the most striking findings from our cross-client analysis: **the gap between the top-quintile and median-quintile charitable raffle is growing**, not shrinking, even though the platforms are commoditizing.

Why? Because the winning factors are increasingly operational:

- Is there a dedicated weekly operating rhythm?
- Is marketing cadence disciplined and measured?
- Is prize architecture optimized against conversion data?
- Is customer support staffed for peak periods?
- Is post-cycle iteration rigorous?

Charities with mature operating capability (whether in-house or through an experienced agency partner) are capturing a disproportionate share of the industry's growth. Charities with immature operations are plateauing at much lower revenue ceilings — even on identical platforms.

## What to expect in 2026–2028

Our read of the trajectory:

**1. U.S. online raffle expansion.** We expect 3–5 more U.S. states to legalize online raffles in the next 24 months. The legislative momentum is clear.

**2. Platform consolidation.** Expect 2–3 more major M&A events as capital flows into the space. The economics of running a full-stack raffle platform favor scale.

**3. AI-driven marketing.** Personalization, send-time optimization, creative generation — the iGaming world has been here for years. Charity raffles are 18 months behind and catching up.

**4. Pressure on regional operators.** Mid-tier platforms without national registrations will face margin compression as enterprise platforms scale down-market.

**5. Cross-border programs.** As U.S. states open up, expect Canadian charities with strong cross-border supporter bases (professional sports, large diaspora communities) to launch unified U.S.–Canada programs.

**6. Regulatory maturation.** More states and provinces will publish dedicated Catch the Ace and progressive raffle guidelines. Expect the "gray area" of 2021 to be a "clearly defined area" by 2028.

## Strategic implications for charity leadership

If you sit on a charity board or lead a foundation, three questions deserve strategic attention in the next 12 months:

1. **Are we in the game at all?** If your organization isn't operating any electronic raffle program, the opportunity cost is now meaningfully large.

2. **If we're in the game, are we operating at top-quintile execution?** If not, what investment — in staff, partners, or technology — would close the gap?

3. **Are we prepared for cross-border expansion?** If your supporter base extends beyond your current jurisdiction, is your program architecture ready to scale when new regulatory doors open?
