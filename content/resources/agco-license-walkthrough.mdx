## AGCO License Application Walkthrough

A plain-English, section-by-section guide to preparing a clean AGCO raffle license application. Written by someone who has personally submitted dozens.

This walkthrough complements our [public post on AGCO licensing](/blog/how-to-get-your-agco-raffle-license-ontario). Use both together: the public post explains the *why*, this walkthrough handles the *how*.

### Section 1 — Organization profile

**What the AGCO wants to see:** clear evidence that your organization is eligible, has been operating in Ontario for at least one year, and has governance structures in good order.

What to include:

- Letters patent or articles of incorporation (certified copies).
- Current bylaws.
- Current list of directors and officers with roles.
- Most recent audited financial statements or T3010 filing.
- Proof of good standing with Corporations Canada / Ontario.

**Common mistake:** submitting out-of-date bylaws. If your bylaws have been amended, attach the amended version, not the original from 1998.

### Section 2 — Eligible purposes

**What they want:** specific alignment between your charitable mandate and the AGCO's eligible uses (relief of poverty, advancement of education, advancement of religion, other charitable purposes beneficial to the community).

What to include:

- Excerpt from your bylaws showing charitable purposes.
- Brief plain-language statement of your mandate (2–3 sentences).
- Example programs or services funded in the past 12 months.

**Common mistake:** vague charitable purpose language. "Community development" alone is not enough; name the specific programs.

### Section 3 — Raffle program description

The biggest section. The AGCO reviews the actual mechanics of what you're proposing.

What to include:

- Format (50/50, Catch the Ace, calendar, other) and rationale.
- Ticket pricing and tier structure.
- Prize structure: grand, early-bird, secondary (with specific amounts or percentages).
- Cycle length and start/end dates.
- Expected total ticket sales and total prize payout.
- Draw mechanics (RNG spec, live draw procedure, audit trail).
- Geographic scope (Ontario residents only is cleanest; cross-boundary programs require additional scrutiny).

**Common mistake:** describing only the 50/50 mechanics and leaving early-bird and secondary prize draws as "TBD." The AGCO wants all prize mechanics specified up front.

### Section 4 — Use of proceeds

What proceeds will fund. This must be specific.

What to include:

- Line-item budget showing allocation of expected net proceeds.
- Specific programs or initiatives (not general admin).
- Timeline for when proceeds will be deployed (typically within 12 months of cycle close).
- Accountability mechanism for reporting back to supporters on fund use.

**Common mistake:** "Proceeds will support our mission." Always too vague. Name the specific hospital wing, the specific program, the specific cohort of beneficiaries.

### Section 5 — Platform & supplier

If using a commercial platform (almost always), they need to be AGCO-registered as a Gaming-Related Supplier.

What to include:

- Platform name and their current AGCO registration number.
- Copy of your engagement agreement with the platform.
- Platform's RNG certification (they should have this on file).
- Description of where customer data is stored and who has access.

**Common mistake:** including a platform that is not currently AGCO-registered, or whose registration has lapsed. Verify before submitting.

### Section 6 — Financial controls

Evidence that the money will be tracked and safeguarded properly.

What to include:

- Description of dedicated raffle bank account (recommended, not always required).
- Reconciliation cadence (daily or weekly).
- Signing authority for prize payouts.
- Internal audit or external audit plan.
- Prize insurance or reserve documentation (for programs where prize value is fixed).

**Common mistake:** mixing raffle proceeds with general operating funds. The AGCO may not strictly require separation but cleanly separated accounts make ongoing reporting dramatically easier.

### Section 7 — Marketing plan

Often underweighted in applications. The AGCO wants to see that your marketing plan matches your revenue projection.

What to include:

- Channels you'll use (email, social, paid, PR, local media).
- Budget allocation by channel.
- Compliance notes (no targeting of minors, no aggressive tactics, clear odds disclosure).
- Sample creative if available.

**Common mistake:** projecting $1M in ticket sales with a $2,000 marketing budget. The numbers need to be consistent.

### Section 8 — Draw procedures

The actual operational procedure for conducting a draw.

What to include:

- Step-by-step draw procedure.
- Personnel present (and their independence).
- RNG or draw equipment specification.
- Audit documentation (recording, timestamps, witnesses).
- Winner verification and contact procedure.
- Prize payout timeline.

**Common mistake:** describing the draw procedure vaguely or leaving audit documentation as "will be documented." The AGCO wants to see the specific mechanism before approval.

### Section 9 — Reporting commitment

What you will file back to the AGCO after the cycle closes.

What to include:

- Commitment to submit post-cycle report within the AGCO's required window.
- Format and content of the report (total ticket sales, prize payout, net proceeds, use-of-proceeds confirmation).
- Records retention commitment.

**Common mistake:** treating reporting as an afterthought. The AGCO is most concerned about what happens *after* the license is issued. Showing that you understand the reporting burden builds confidence.

---

## Timeline reality check

For a first-time applicant with a well-prepared package: expect 12–18 weeks from eligibility review to final approval. Plan accordingly. Do not promise your board a launch date that assumes a rushed application.
