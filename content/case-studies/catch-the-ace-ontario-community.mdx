## The Challenge

Thunder Bay Regional Health Sciences Foundation had already built the largest hospital 50/50 in Canada. The cycles were massive — but they were discrete. Between 50/50 cycles, supporter engagement went quiet. No tickets to buy, no weekly ritual, no milestone moments.

The foundation's board wanted to know: could a second, parallel program keep supporters engaged through the quiet windows — without cannibalizing the flagship 50/50?

## The Approach

We designed Thunder Bay Catch the Ace as a deliberate complement to the 50/50 — not a competitor.

**Different rhythm, same brand.** The 50/50 operates in cycles of several weeks. Catch the Ace runs weekly, year-round. Supporters could participate in both without experiencing the fatigue of two overlapping sales pushes for the same cause.

**Different ticket size.** The 50/50's ticket-buyer is typically buying $40–$100 at a time in a focused cycle. Catch the Ace supporters buy $10–$20 per week, consistently. Same cause, different budget mindset, different share-of-wallet.

**Unified supporter experience.** A single unified account system meant that a supporter who loved the 50/50 could seamlessly also participate in Catch the Ace without creating a new account, re-entering a payment method, or opting into a new email list. Friction was removed at every seam.

**Live weekly draw as community ritual.** The weekly Catch the Ace draw became a community event — broadcast live, with local personalities, hosted publicly. It reinforced the brand connection every single week, not just during 50/50 cycles.

**Licensing and operational alignment.** Both programs operated under complementary AGCO licenses, with prize structures and operational controls designed to meet the distinct regulatory requirements of a progressive raffle vs. a traditional 50/50.

The most important finding — and the one we're asked about most often by charities considering parallel programs — is that the two programs measurably made *each other* bigger. The 50/50's cycles actually performed better in quarters when Catch the Ace was active. Supporters who engaged weekly with Catch the Ace were more likely to buy 50/50 tickets, at higher average basket sizes, when the 50/50 cycle launched.

## What Made It Work

**1. Explicit role differentiation.** Every design decision reinforced that the two programs served different supporter needs. No confusion about "which one do I buy tickets for?" because they answered different questions.

**2. Unified identity.** From the supporter's perspective, both programs shared a single brand, single account, and single trust relationship. The operational complexity of running two programs was hidden.

**3. Data integration.** Cross-program supporter data meant we could see exactly how the two programs interacted. When we noticed (in the data) that CTA supporters were converting to 50/50 buyers at an unusually high rate, we built onboarding flows specifically to accelerate that conversion.

**4. Year-round rhythm.** The biggest structural benefit of the parallel program was that there was never a "dead period" in supporter engagement. The charity was always in front of its supporters, with something meaningful to engage with.

## The Takeaway

For charities that have reached a certain scale with a primary raffle program, running a second, complementary program is one of the most underutilized levers in charitable gaming. The math only works, though, when:

- The two programs are deliberately differentiated in format, rhythm, and ticket-size.
- The supporter experience is genuinely unified across programs.
- The operational and regulatory complexity of running two parallel programs is managed (which is non-trivial).
- The charity is willing to invest in the year-round cadence — it's a significant operational commitment.

Thunder Bay's model isn't the only way to do it. But the principle — that a well-designed parallel program grows both programs — is reproducible.
