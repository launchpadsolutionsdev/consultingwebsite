## The Challenge

Community Foundations of America (CFAC) had built a solid raffle program but had hit a revenue ceiling. Traditional 50/50 cycles were plateauing at a level well below what the foundation needed to fund its expanding community programs.

The board explored alternative models. One hypothesis: a **prize-driven raffle** — where the prize is a specific high-value physical item (a vehicle, in this case) rather than a cash-pot split — could unlock a different supporter persona and expand total fundraising capacity.

## The Approach

We designed a Jeep raffle alongside the existing 50/50 cycles, specifically structured to *not* cannibalize the flagship program.

**Physical, aspirational prize.** A fully-loaded Jeep Wrangler is tangible in a way that "half of an unknown jackpot" isn't. It activates a different buyer psychology: supporters who wouldn't engage with a cash raffle responded enthusiastically to the vehicle — especially in the regions where off-road lifestyle brands have cultural weight.

**Different sales window, different supporter base.** The Jeep raffle was timed to *not* overlap with 50/50 cycles. Supporters who bought 50/50 tickets also bought Jeep tickets, but in separate transactions with separate budget allocations. The two programs were additive, not substitutive.

**Prize insurance and financial controls.** Unlike a 50/50 (where the prize scales with ticket sales), a prize-driven raffle must have the prize guaranteed from cycle start. We structured prize insurance and financial reserves to cover the prize regardless of ticket sales performance, satisfying both AGCO requirements and board risk comfort.

**Storytelling-first marketing.** The Jeep wasn't sold as "a car." It was sold as an adventure, a lifestyle, a symbol. Marketing leaned heavily into photo and video content featuring the vehicle in the landscapes its eventual winner would drive it through.

**Regional targeting.** Paid marketing concentrated on regions where Jeep's cultural brand is strong (rural, outdoorsy, truck-and-SUV dominant markets) rather than spending broadly.

The most important result isn't the dollar figure — it's that 42% of Jeep raffle ticket buyers had **never previously donated or bought a raffle ticket** from the foundation. The physical prize unlocked a new supporter segment that the cash-pot 50/50 had been unable to reach. Many of those new supporters subsequently converted to 50/50 ticket buyers in later cycles.

## What Made It Work

**1. Different psychology, different buyer.** The structural insight that a physical prize attracts a different supporter persona than a cash pot is under-appreciated in the industry. Leveraging it deliberately unlocked real growth.

**2. Careful program timing.** Running the Jeep raffle *between* 50/50 cycles (rather than in parallel) prevented cannibalization and allowed full marketing focus on each program at its moment.

**3. Prize as a brand asset.** The Jeep, and the imagery around it, became part of the foundation's broader brand — content that worked on social media, in press, and in community engagement, beyond just the raffle sales funnel.

**4. New supporter acquisition.** The Jeep raffle was explicitly designed as a **supporter acquisition engine**, not just a revenue event. That framing changed how the team measured success and what they optimized for.

## The Takeaway

For charities whose 50/50s have plateaued — and who are looking for structural growth rather than incremental — a well-designed prize raffle can be a powerful second program. The preconditions:

- A prize with genuine cultural weight in your community (not a generic "electronic package").
- Willingness to carry prize risk (through insurance or reserves) rather than having the prize scale with ticket sales.
- A sales window that doesn't overlap with your primary raffle.
- A regional marketing strategy rather than broad digital.
- A plan for converting net-new ticket buyers into long-term supporters.

When those pieces are in place, prize raffles complement — rather than compete with — 50/50 programs.
