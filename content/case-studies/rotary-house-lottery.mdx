## The Challenge

Rotary clubs run on volunteer energy, and for decades that energy was split roughly 50/50 between community service and fundraising events — pancake breakfasts, car washes, golf tournaments, silent auctions. The math was exhausting: dozens of small events, each requiring weeks of volunteer labor, netting a few thousand dollars apiece.

The club's leadership had a question: could we consolidate all of that fundraising effort into a single, well-run raffle program, and redirect the freed-up volunteer hours toward actual community service?

## The Approach

We designed a Catch the Ace program specifically for a service club operating model:

**Right format for the audience.** Catch the Ace's weekly rhythm aligned naturally with how Rotary members engage with their community — weekly club meetings, weekly social rituals, weekly touchpoints. A 50/50 with a one-time-per-month draw would have fought against that rhythm; Catch the Ace leveraged it.

**Volunteer-light operations.** The program was designed so that day-to-day operations required minimal volunteer labor. The platform handled ticket sales, reconciliation, and winner notification. Volunteer energy was reserved for the weekly live draw (which doubled as a community engagement event) and strategic marketing.

**Local-first marketing.** Rather than broad digital marketing, the program leaned into Rotary's existing strength: community relationships. Local business partnerships, local media, local event cross-promotion. Every Rotary member became a micro-ambassador.

**Full license + compliance support.** The club didn't have dedicated staff to navigate AGCO licensing. Our team handled application preparation, ongoing compliance reporting, and regulatory coordination end-to-end.

The numbers are meaningful in aggregate, but the real transformation is structural. The club has retired three of its legacy fundraising events entirely — events that were exhausting to run and yielded mediocre returns. The volunteer hours that used to go into pancake flipping now go into building a youth literacy program and a community accessibility initiative.

## What Made It Work

**1. Matched format to community rhythm.** Catch the Ace's weekly cycle aligned with how Rotary already operated. A format that required a different operating cadence would have failed.

**2. Leveraged existing community assets.** The club's greatest asset is its member network and its local relationships. The program was designed to *use* those assets, not compete with them.

**3. Full-service partnership.** A volunteer-led organization cannot realistically run licensing, ongoing compliance, and platform operations on its own. The decision to partner fully — not just use a platform self-serve — was what made the program sustainable.

**4. Redirected, not added, labor.** This was not additional fundraising. It replaced lower-yielding, higher-effort activities. The club's total fundraising volunteer hours *decreased* while revenue increased.

## The Takeaway

Service clubs — Rotary, Lions, Kiwanis, Knights of Columbus, and many others — are often sitting on enormous untapped fundraising potential. The traditional event model is exhausting, yields modest returns, and takes volunteer energy away from service. A well-run raffle program can invert the equation.

The critical question isn't whether a service club *can* run a successful raffle. It's whether the club is willing to commit to:
- Retiring lower-yielding events to free up capacity.
- Partnering for operational support, not trying to run everything in-house.
- Maintaining a consistent weekly cadence for 6–12 months minimum.

When those commitments are in place, the results replicate.
