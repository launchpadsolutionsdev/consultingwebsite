When a charity considers running an electronic raffle for the first time, the choice usually comes down to two formats: **a 50/50 raffle** or **Catch the Ace**. Both are progressive. Both have produced multi-million-dollar jackpots. Both run on similar platforms.

They are not, however, interchangeable.

Choosing the wrong format for your supporter base, your geography, and your operating capacity is one of the single biggest unforced errors we see charities make. Here's how we help organizations decide.

## The mechanics, in plain English

### 50/50 Raffle
A 50/50 runs over a **fixed period** (typically a month, a quarter, or an event window). Ticket sales accumulate into a jackpot. At the draw date, a single winner receives 50% of the total pot; the charity keeps the other 50% plus any operating margin. Then the cycle resets.

### Catch the Ace
Catch the Ace is a **progressive, weekly-draw** raffle. Tickets are sold all week; at the weekly draw, a single ticket-holder is selected and gets to choose an envelope from a board of 52 playing cards (one per card). If they pick the Ace of Spades, they win the progressive jackpot. If they don't, they win a weekly consolation prize, the card is removed, and the jackpot rolls into next week.

Because the jackpot can roll over for weeks or months — the Ace of Spades is only found, on average, in week 26 of a full deck — the final weeks become a viral frenzy.

## The supporter experience

This is the most overlooked difference between the two formats.

A 50/50 is a single, focused moment: buy tickets, wait for the draw, check the winning number. Supporters engage once per cycle. If your cycle is monthly, you have 12 engagement moments per year per supporter.

Catch the Ace is a **weekly ritual**. Supporters check in every Thursday (or whatever your draw night is) to see if the ace was caught, who the weekly winner was, and how much the jackpot grew. The emotional rhythm is closer to a TV series than a one-time ask.

  50/50s are fireworks. Catch the Ace is a slow-burning campfire that occasionally roars into flames. Both can draw a crowd — but they draw different crowds, at different times, in different ways.
</PullQuote>

## The revenue profile

50/50s are more predictable. You set a cycle length, plan your marketing spend against it, and you have a reasonable forecast of what the jackpot will close at.

Catch the Ace is wildly non-linear. The first 10–15 weeks build slowly as the jackpot grows. Weeks 20–35 are the sweet spot where virality kicks in. The final weeks — especially once the board is down to 3–5 cards — can see a single week out-earn the first three months combined.

## The operational burden

A 50/50 is operationally lighter. One licensing application per cycle, one marketing campaign, one draw event. You can run two or three a year.

Catch the Ace requires a **weekly operating rhythm**. Every single week, for up to a year:
- A live draw must be conducted and recorded.
- A winner must be verified, contacted, and paid.
- The card board must be updated (and the unopened envelope audited).
- A weekly report must be filed.
- A marketing push must ship before the next week's draw.

Miss a week, and the regulator takes notice.

## The regulatory picture (Ontario specifically)

In Ontario, Catch the Ace is regulated under the AGCO's progressive raffle framework. Licensing is typically **municipal** (for prizes under a set threshold) and **provincial** (above it). The rules have tightened meaningfully in recent years — the AGCO now issues a comprehensive Catch the Ace guide and requires specific operating controls on the card board, the drum, and the live draw.

50/50 raffles under $50,000 in total prizes can be licensed at the municipal level in most Ontario municipalities. Above that, you're looking at a provincial license with stricter reporting.

If your charity operates across multiple provinces or states, the licensing picture gets significantly more complex — this is the #1 area where we see in-house programs make costly mistakes. ## A decision framework

| Factor | Choose 50/50 | Choose Catch the Ace |
|---|---|---|
| Operating capacity | Limited bandwidth for weekly ops | Can sustain weekly cadence for 6–12 months |
| Supporter base | Medium-large, infrequent engagement | Loyal, community-oriented, weekly engagement |
| Geography | Any jurisdiction that permits raffles | Strongest in Ontario, Atlantic Canada, emerging elsewhere |
| Revenue predictability | High predictability preferred | Comfortable with non-linear, long-tail revenue |
| Marketing resources | Can run focused campaigns | Can sustain weekly content + email cadence |
| Brand tolerance | Traditional charity brand | Brand can support a "game show" vibe |

## The "why not both?" answer

For charities with the operating capacity, the best programs often run **both**. A 50/50 cycle every quarter drives short-term revenue spikes. A parallel Catch the Ace provides the steady weekly engagement that keeps supporters in the habit of checking in.

Thunder Bay Regional Health Sciences Foundation — the organization behind the largest hospital 50/50 in Canada — is a textbook example. Their 50/50 provides the headline-grabbing jackpots. Their Catch the Ace keeps supporters engaged in the in-between weeks. Neither program cannibalizes the other; they feed each other's supporter lists.

## The bottom line

There is no universally "better" format. The best raffle is the one your team can actually run well, that your supporter base will actually engage with, and that fits the regulatory landscape where you operate.
