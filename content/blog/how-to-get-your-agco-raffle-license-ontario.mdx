If your charity operates in Ontario and you're planning to run an electronic raffle, you need a license. This post walks through what that process actually looks like — not the sanitized version from the AGCO website, but the practical, experience-based version that tells you where things actually get stuck.

<Callout type="info">
  This article is a practical walkthrough, not legal advice. Every charity's situation is different. For binding guidance on your specific program, consult a lawyer experienced in Ontario charitable gaming law — or, for an easier path, a raffle management partner who handles licensing as part of their service.
</Callout>

## Who licenses what

In Ontario, raffle licensing is split across two authorities:

- **Municipalities** license raffles with total prize value **up to $50,000**. Each city or township has its own process and form — and they vary widely in responsiveness and familiarity with electronic raffles.
- **The AGCO (Alcohol and Gaming Commission of Ontario)** licenses raffles with total prize value **above $50,000**, plus any electronic raffle that crosses municipal boundaries or is conducted over the internet.

For any serious electronic 50/50 or Catch the Ace program, assume you need a **provincial (AGCO) license**, not a municipal one.

## Step 1: Eligibility

Before the AGCO will issue a license, your organization must be:

1. An **eligible charitable or religious organization** — the definition is narrower than "has a charitable registration number." The AGCO's eligibility standard covers organizations whose mandate is the relief of poverty, the advancement of education, the advancement of religion, or other charitable objects that benefit the community.
2. Operating in Ontario for **at least one year** before applying. Newly incorporated charities typically need to wait.
3. Using the proceeds for a **specified eligible use** — typically programs and services that serve the community, not general administration.

If your organization has never been licensed by the AGCO for any form of gaming before, you'll need to submit an eligibility review package *before* you can apply for any specific raffle. Budget 6–10 weeks for this.

## Step 2: Program design (before you submit anything)

This is the step most charities shortcut — and pay for later. Before you fill out a single form, you need a clear, written plan covering:

- **Format and cadence.** 50/50, Catch the Ace, calendar raffle, or other. Weekly, monthly, or continuous.
- **Prize structure.** Grand prize mechanism, secondary prizes, early-bird prizes (with specific ticket-sales thresholds).
- **Ticket sales channels.** Online only, online + in-person, point-of-sale at events, etc.
- **Geographic scope.** Ontario residents only, cross-province, cross-border.
- **Platform.** Which provider, who hosts it, where customer data is stored.
- **Draw mechanics.** RNG specification, live-draw procedures, audit trail.
- **Financial controls.** Bank account separation, reconciliation cadence, reporting cadence.
- **Marketing plan.** Channels, messaging, spending.
- **Use of proceeds.** Specifically which charitable programs will receive funding.

The AGCO evaluates applications against this plan. Vagueness is a delay trigger.

## Step 3: The application itself

The AGCO raffle license application requires:

- **Charitable organization details** (incorporation documents, bylaws, list of directors, audited financial statements).
- **Full program description** (everything from Step 2, in the AGCO's required format).
- **Platform and system specifications** (often requires the gaming provider to submit their own supplier documentation — most established platforms have this pre-packaged).
- **Use of proceeds plan** with specific budget allocations.
- **Draw procedures** with clear RNG and audit controls.
- **Prize insurance or payment guarantees** (to ensure winners are paid regardless of ticket sales performance).

The application itself is relatively mechanical if you've done Step 2 well. If you haven't, you'll be sent back to redo sections — and each round trip is 3–6 weeks.

## Step 4: Supplier registration

If you're using a commercial raffle platform (which you almost certainly are), that platform must be **registered with the AGCO as a Gaming-Related Supplier**. Not every provider is.

This is a crucial vetting point. Before you commit to a platform, confirm they are currently AGCO-registered. Unregistered platforms cannot legally operate Ontario raffles — no matter how polished their sales pitch is.

## Step 5: Timelines (realistic, not aspirational)

For a first-time applicant with a well-prepared package:

- **Eligibility review**: 6–10 weeks (if not previously licensed)
- **Raffle application review**: 4–8 weeks
- **Total, first-time applicant**: ~12–18 weeks from scratch

For a repeat applicant with a clean history:

- **Raffle application review**: 3–6 weeks
- **Total, returning applicant**: 4–8 weeks

<Callout type="warning">
  If you're planning a launch for a specific event or date (an anniversary, a major fundraising push, a season opener), start the licensing process **at least six months in advance**. Organizations that try to compress this process routinely miss their launch date.
</Callout>

## Step 6: Ongoing compliance

The license is not the end. Once you're running, you have ongoing obligations:

- **Reporting**: Post-cycle reports within the AGCO's specified window (typically 30 days of cycle close).
- **Use of proceeds**: Documented evidence that proceeds were used as described in your application.
- **Prize payouts**: Within AGCO-mandated timelines, with documentation.
- **Record retention**: Full financial and ticketing records retained for a specified period.
- **Changes**: Any material changes to format, platform, prize structure, or use of proceeds require AGCO notification and often re-approval.

Compliance issues compound. A single late report rarely kills a license, but a pattern of late reports is how licenses get suspended.

## Common delays (and how to avoid them)

Based on dozens of applications we've helped charities prepare:

1. **Vague use-of-proceeds language.** Fix: specify exactly which programs will receive funding, with line-item budgets.
2. **Missing supplier documentation.** Fix: confirm your platform has current AGCO registration before including them.
3. **Inconsistent numbers across documents.** Fix: build a single master spreadsheet and copy numbers from it into every form.
4. **Unclear draw mechanics.** Fix: diagram the draw procedure step-by-step, including RNG specification, and have your platform provider review.
5. **Prize guarantee gaps.** Fix: have either prize insurance or a clearly documented guarantee from your organization's reserves, with proof of funds.

## Cross-border and multi-jurisdiction programs

If your charity serves beneficiaries outside Ontario — or if you want to sell tickets to supporters outside Ontario — the picture gets significantly more complex. Each province has its own framework; each U.S. state that permits raffles has its own. Most charities running cross-border programs work with a raffle management partner specifically because managing 5–13 concurrent licenses is a full-time operational function.

## The punchline

The AGCO process is rigorous but not mysterious. Charities that invest in a clean, well-designed program *before* they start the application pass through in 4–8 weeks. Charities that try to shortcut the design phase and improvise during the application process routinely burn 4–6 months — and sometimes have to start over entirely.

Do the work up front. Build the program clearly. Submit a clean application. The rest is waiting — and executing.
