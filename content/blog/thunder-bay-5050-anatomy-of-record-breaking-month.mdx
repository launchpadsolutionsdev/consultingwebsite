In early 2024, the Thunder Bay Regional Health Sciences Foundation closed out a 50/50 cycle with a jackpot that made national headlines: **$15.7 million in ticket sales in a single month**, a $7.85M prize to the winning ticket-holder, and a record-breaking payout to hospital programs serving a region of over 250,000 people.

It wasn't the first time Thunder Bay had led the country. But it was the moment that the rest of the charitable sector started paying attention.

Having been part of the team on the ground, here's the honest breakdown of what drove that number — and, more importantly, what parts of the playbook *any* charity can learn from.

## The setup

By the time of the record-breaking cycle, Thunder Bay's 50/50 had been running for several years. Each cycle, the team tightened another piece of the operation: the email flow got better, the landing page got faster, the jackpot trajectory got more predictable, and the brand became something Thunder Bay residents *expected*.

The region itself is uniquely suited: population of ~108,000 in the city, ~250,000 in the broader catchment, one major regional hospital serving all of Northwestern Ontario. Virtually every family in the region has a connection to the hospital — a birth, a surgery, cancer care, an emergency room visit. That emotional resonance is the foundation every dollar was built on.

## What actually drove the number

There is no single lever. There are about fifteen of them, compounding.

### 1. Prize architecture

The program didn't just dangle the grand prize. It layered:
- **A grand prize 50/50 jackpot** that grew throughout the cycle.
- **Early-bird prizes** that incentivized buyers to purchase early rather than wait for the final week.
- **Secondary "bonus" draws** at specific ticket sales thresholds.

Each additional prize tier measurably increased ticket-buyer conversion and average order value. The lift from well-designed tiers is reliably 20–40% on top of a flat-structure equivalent.

### 2. Marketing cadence with discipline

During the cycle, the marketing calendar ran like clockwork:
- Email to the full list weekly, plus triggered flows on cart abandonment and reactivation.
- Social organic posts daily, with a different creative angle each week.
- Paid social with geographic targeting (Thunder Bay + Northwestern Ontario first, then expanding).
- Press releases timed to jackpot milestones ($1M, $5M, $10M).
- Radio, with a mix of local stations, running heavier in the final two weeks.

None of those channels individually moved the number dramatically. Stacked together, sustained for four weeks, they compounded.

### 3. Press leverage

Every jackpot milestone became a news story. CTV, CBC, TB Newswatch — local and national outlets picked up every major threshold. Each story sent a fresh wave of new buyers to the site.

This isn't luck; it's a deliberate PR calendar. Milestones are planned, press releases are pre-drafted, and reporters are briefed in advance. By the time $10M is hit, the story writes itself.

### 4. Real customer support

When a raffle is pushing $500K a day in ticket sales, the support load is enormous. The tiniest friction — an error on checkout, a question about whether the ticket went through, a concern about prize payout — can tank a 30-minute window of revenue if it's not answered fast.

The Thunder Bay program had humans on chat, email, and phone during peak sales hours. Not an AI bot. Real people who understood the program and could resolve issues in minutes.

### 5. Obsessive data discipline

Every cycle, the team reviewed:
- Revenue-by-hour curves.
- Conversion rates by traffic source.
- Email deliverability and engagement.
- Prize tier conversion deltas.
- Geographic concentration of buyers.

Then they changed one or two things for the next cycle — and measured the delta.

## What any charity can learn

Your charity is probably not about to do $15.7M in a month. But the principles that drove Thunder Bay's number are scale-invariant:

**1. Build around your community's emotional anchor.** Thunder Bay's hospital is irreplaceable in its region. What's irreplaceable about your charity? That's the single most important piece of messaging.

**2. Layer your prize structure.** Never run a flat grand-prize-only raffle. Always have early-bird and threshold prizes.

**3. Run a disciplined weekly marketing cadence.** Pick three channels, execute them relentlessly, and measure.

**4. Stack milestones into press moments.** Every $100K, $500K, $1M threshold is a press opportunity. Pre-write the releases.

**5. Treat support as revenue.** Every unanswered question is a lost sale. Staff accordingly during hot periods.

**6. Review, iterate, repeat.** The charities whose numbers plateau are the ones who run the exact same cycle every time. The ones that grow treat each cycle as a beta test for the next one.

## Why this matters beyond Thunder Bay

The larger lesson is about what's possible — and how unevenly distributed the know-how is.

Most charities running raffles are stuck in a "set it and forget it" mindset. They pick a platform, launch, and hope. A small number — the ones that break records — treat raffles as a product, invest in operational capability, and iterate relentlessly.

The gap between those two approaches is measured in tens of millions of dollars, per cycle.
