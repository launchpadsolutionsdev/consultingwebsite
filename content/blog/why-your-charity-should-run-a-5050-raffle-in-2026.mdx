## The industry is finally big enough to matter

For a long time, the charitable gaming industry sat quietly in the background of the fundraising world. It was mostly paper tickets at hockey games and rubber-banded rolls sold at silent auctions. Then Thunder Bay Regional Health Sciences Foundation, Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation, and a handful of NHL and CFL team foundations started running online 50/50s — and the numbers turned into headlines.

## Why 50/50s outperform most alternatives

A 50/50 raffle splits the jackpot with a winning ticket-holder — half to the winner, half to the cause. That simple mechanic drives outcomes that are hard to replicate:

**The incentive is aligned with virality.** The bigger the pot grows, the more people want a ticket, and the faster word spreads. Social reach becomes a compounding curve, not a linear one.

**It's a low-friction ask.** A $10 ticket is not a donation. It's a moment of hope. Donors who'd never commit to a monthly gift will gladly buy five tickets — and many convert to recurring supporters later.

**It's repeatable.** Unlike a gala that requires 6 months of prep for a one-night event, a well-designed raffle program runs on a cadence — monthly, weekly, or continuously — and builds on itself each cycle.

**The math is forgiving.** Even modest raffles net 30–45% after prize payout and operating costs. Elite programs push net margins higher by stacking secondary prizes, progressive add-ons, and subscription-style recurring ticket purchases.

## Who should (and shouldn't) run one

A 50/50 is a force multiplier — but only for organizations that meet a few baseline conditions.

### Good fit
- You have an engaged community (event attendees, newsletter subscribers, or social followers) — even a modest one.
- Your cause has emotional resonance. Hospitals, sports franchises, first responders, and local community charities over-index here.
- You can commit to a weekly operating rhythm for at least 6 months.
- You have — or can partner with — someone who owns licensing and compliance.

### Less good fit (right now)
- Ultra-small organizations without a staff member who can own the weekly cadence.
- Organizations in jurisdictions where online raffles remain restricted (see our [regulatory landscape report](/insights/electronic-raffles-by-state-regulatory-landscape)).
- Charities whose supporter base is extremely quiet on digital channels and who aren't willing to invest in building one.

## The "run in-house vs. hire a manager" decision

This is where most charities get it wrong. They assume running a raffle is fundamentally a *technology* problem — pick a platform, and you're in business.

It isn't. It's an operations problem. Platforms are commoditized; operational excellence isn't.

The cost of running a raffle poorly is brutal: a licensing slip-up pulls your program; a slow support response during a hot jackpot week kneecaps revenue; a weak marketing plan means the jackpot never reaches critical mass. We break down the full math in [The Real Cost of Running an In-House Raffle vs. Hiring a Management Agency](/blog/in-house-vs-agency-raffle-management), but the short version: unless you have a dedicated raffle operator with charitable gaming experience on staff, you're almost always better off with a partner.

## What a great first program looks like

If your board is asking whether to explore a raffle, here's the honest, experience-based answer about what the first 12 months should look like:

1. **Months 1–2**: Licensing, platform selection, prize structure, brand creative, launch marketing plan. Nothing sells yet.
2. **Month 3**: Soft launch. A short first draw (2–4 weeks) to learn the mechanics. Expect a modest result. That's fine.
3. **Months 4–6**: Iterate. Tighten the email cadence. Add SMS. Test a progressive add-on like Catch the Ace.
4. **Months 7–12**: Scale. Move from monthly to bi-weekly draws. Layer in subscription purchases. Optimize prize tiers against your actual conversion data.

Charities that follow this arc — and are patient through the first six months — routinely 3x–5x their initial draw by month 12. The ones who expect to beat Thunder Bay's numbers in round one are the ones who quit at month 3.
