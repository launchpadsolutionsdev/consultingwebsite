Ask ten charities why their raffle isn't growing, and nine will point at the platform, the prize, or the marketing budget. The tenth — the one whose raffle *is* growing — will tell you the truth: **the single biggest predictor of raffle revenue is the size and quality of your supporter list.**

Your platform is a tool. Your prize is a draw. Your marketing is a flywheel. But your supporter list is the infrastructure all three run on. Every dollar you spend acquiring an engaged supporter today is a dollar that compounds across every future cycle.

## Why the list matters more than the platform

Consider two identical raffles, run on identical platforms, with identical prize structures. The only variable: one organization has 15,000 engaged email subscribers; the other has 2,000.

In our experience, the first organization will raise **3–5x** the second one. Not because it markets better. Because every email send reaches 7.5x more people, at roughly the same unit cost per cycle.

## The three tiers of a supporter list

Not every contact is equal. A rigorous program tracks three tiers:

### Tier 1: Ticket buyers (the gold)
People who have bought at least one raffle ticket. These are your highest-value contacts — they've already demonstrated intent, have a payment method on file, and trust your brand. In aggregate, Tier 1 contacts drive 60–75% of cycle revenue. Protect them.

### Tier 2: Engaged subscribers (the silver)
People on your email list who open, click, and engage but haven't converted yet. Every cycle, a percentage of these convert to Tier 1. Your job is to make that percentage grow.

### Tier 3: Latent audience (the bronze)
Social followers, event attendees, cause-sympathizers who haven't given you an email. Not directly monetizable, but valuable as a conversion surface. Moving a Tier 3 contact to Tier 2 is the leading indicator of list growth.

## Where supporter lists actually come from

The honest answer: from *other* things your charity does well. Lists don't grow because you have a signup form on your homepage. They grow because you're running activities that people want to be part of.

**From live events.** Every gala, community run, hospital open house, or youth sports tournament your charity is involved in is a list-building opportunity. Put a QR code on every table. Give away a branded item in exchange for an email. Stream event recaps to the signup page.

**From the raffle itself.** Every ticket buyer should opt into your newsletter as part of checkout (with clear consent, complying with CASL / CAN-SPAM). Your Tier 1 list grows automatically — but only if the checkout flow is designed to capture that opt-in.

**From content.** Publish articles, tip sheets, and behind-the-scenes updates that your community actually wants to read. People give you their email in exchange for something useful. This article you're reading right now is an example.

**From partnerships.** Cross-promotional relationships with other aligned nonprofits, local businesses, or community organizations. Co-host a live event; co-promote a giveaway; co-sponsor content.

**From paid acquisition.** Meta and Google ads driving to a lead-magnet landing page. This only makes sense once you've optimized the organic channels first — otherwise the CAC is too high.

## The email-quality trap

Bigger lists are not inherently better. A list of 20,000 cold, unengaged contacts is worse than a list of 3,000 engaged ones — because the 20,000 will drag down your deliverability rates, trigger spam filters, and reduce the open rate on every send for everyone.

Great programs are ruthless about **list hygiene**:
- Contacts who haven't engaged in 6 months get moved to a sunset flow.
- If they don't re-engage, they get removed — not "one last chance" emailed forever.
- Hard bounces are removed immediately.
- Invalid emails are cleaned quarterly with a verification service.

This feels counterintuitive ("I don't want to lose a subscriber!"), but the math works out. A clean 8,000-person list outperforms a bloated 15,000-person list on nearly every meaningful KPI.

## What to actually send

Charities often ask what their pre-raffle, during-raffle, and post-raffle email cadence should look like. Roughly:

**Pre-launch (2–4 weeks out)**
- Teaser announcement ("something big is coming")
- Behind-the-scenes: the cause, the mission, why this raffle matters
- Launch-day-is-here email

**During cycle (weekly)**
- Monday: jackpot update with momentum framing
- Wednesday: beneficiary spotlight (a story about the cause)
- Friday: urgency / draw reminder
- Final week: daily push, escalating urgency

**Post-cycle**
- Winner announcement email (high open rate — use it)
- Thank-you email with cause impact framing
- "Here's what your tickets funded" a few weeks later (builds trust for next cycle)

  The most underrated email in the sequence is the post-cycle "here's what your tickets funded" message. It's the bridge that converts one-time ticket buyers into repeat supporters — and it's the one most charities forget to send.
</Callout>

## SMS is the new email (but smaller)

Most charities underutilize SMS. It has dramatically higher open rates (typically 90%+ vs. 20–35% for email), much lower deliverability problems, and — crucially — it's still not saturated for nonprofits.

The rules of SMS for charities:
1. Opt-in must be explicit and compliant.
2. Send frequency has to be much lower than email (1–2 per cycle, not weekly).
3. Every message must offer real value — a big milestone, a final-hours reminder, a winner announcement.
4. Every message must include an easy opt-out.

Charities that add SMS thoughtfully see a **10–20% lift** in final-week revenue, at near-zero marginal cost.

## The compounding effect

The real power of supporter lists is that they compound. Every cycle, a well-run program:
- Acquires new Tier 1 ticket buyers.
- Moves a percentage of Tier 2 subscribers up to Tier 1.
- Moves a percentage of Tier 3 followers up to Tier 2.

Each tier has a conversion rate to the next. Over 4–8 cycles, a charity that starts with 2,000 subscribers and executes consistently can reach 10,000+ — and that's when the revenue curve takes off.

The charities that panic about revenue plateaus are usually the same ones ignoring the underlying list health. Fix the list; the revenue follows.

## The takeaway

Raffles are not won by platforms. They are won by brands with engaged communities who trust them, hear from them regularly, and want to be part of what they're building. The raffle is simply the most repeatable way for that community to convert its enthusiasm into dollars.

Build the list first. Or rather — keep building it, forever. It's the single asset with the longest-compounding return in nonprofit fundraising.
