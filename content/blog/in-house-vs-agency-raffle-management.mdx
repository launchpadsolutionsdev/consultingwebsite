One of the first questions a charity asks when evaluating a raffle program is: *"Can we just do this ourselves?"*

The answer, almost always, is: **yes, you technically can — but doing it well is harder, more expensive, and riskier than most boards realize.** Here's the honest math, written by a firm that runs raffles for a living. Yes, we're biased. We'll be transparent about that, and you can check our numbers against any other operator you talk to.

## What actually goes into running a raffle

First, a reality check on everything a raffle program requires. If any of this is missing, the program doesn't run:

**Pre-launch (one-time)**
1. Program design (format, cadence, prize structure, financial model)
2. AGCO or state licensing
3. Platform selection and setup
4. Brand design and creative (landing page, ads, email templates)
5. Payment processor setup
6. Financial controls and reporting infrastructure
7. Marketing plan and initial ad creative

**Per-cycle (ongoing)**
1. Weekly (or per-cycle) marketing execution — email, social, paid media, PR
2. Real-time customer support
3. Platform operations and uptime monitoring
4. Draw administration and audit trail
5. Winner verification and prize payout
6. Financial reconciliation
7. Post-cycle reporting (regulatory and internal)
8. Retrospective and optimization for next cycle

Run through that list and ask: *who on my staff does each of these things?* The honest answer usually reveals the real issue.

## The in-house cost model

Let's price it out. Conservatively, for a charity running a credible 50/50 program (targeting >$250K per cycle):

### Year 1 direct costs

| Line item | Estimated cost |
|---|---|
| Raffle platform (licensing + per-ticket fees) | $15,000 – $40,000 |
| Payment processing fees (~2.9% on ticket sales) | $7,000 – $25,000 |
| Legal and licensing fees | $5,000 – $12,000 |
| Initial creative and brand work | $8,000 – $20,000 |
| Cycle marketing (paid, PR, content) | $30,000 – $80,000 |
| Prize insurance (if used) | $3,000 – $10,000 |
| **Direct cost subtotal** | **$68,000 – $187,000** |

### Year 1 personnel costs (the hidden one)

This is where in-house programs get really expensive — and where boards most often underestimate.

| Role | FTE requirement | Annual cost |
|---|---|---|
| Raffle program manager | 0.5 – 1.0 FTE | $50,000 – $100,000 |
| Marketing support | 0.25 FTE | $18,000 |
| Customer support (peak coverage) | ~0.5 FTE effective | $25,000 – $40,000 |
| Finance / compliance | 0.1 FTE | $10,000 |
| **Personnel subtotal** | | **$103,000 – $168,000** |

### Total Year 1 in-house cost: **$171,000 – $355,000**

Now, the catch: **most first-cycle in-house raffles raise $100,000–$400,000 gross**. After prize payout (50%), you're looking at net revenue of $50K–$200K — against costs of $171K–$355K.

For most charities, the first year is a net loss. Year 2 and beyond can be profitable if the program scales and the team has genuinely learned what works. Many programs plateau in year 2 and never grow.

## The agency cost model

Engaging a specialized raffle management partner typically takes one of three forms:

**Flat-fee model**: $X per month or per cycle, independent of revenue. Usually $5K–$15K per month for a full-service engagement.

**Revenue share**: A percentage of ticket revenue or net proceeds. Commonly 10–20% depending on scope.

**Hybrid**: A smaller flat fee plus a performance-based component.

For a realistic comparison to the in-house model above, assume a mid-tier agency engagement at **$120K/year** (flat or hybrid).

### What you get for that, in practice

- Licensing application preparation and ongoing compliance management
- Platform selection, setup, and ongoing operations (often the agency's own or partnered)
- Full marketing execution (email, social, paid, PR)
- Customer support staffing during peak hours
- Draw administration and audit documentation
- Post-cycle reporting and retrospective
- Program optimization based on agency-wide learnings across many clients

### What it saves you (in risk and time)

This is the part boards tend to undervalue:

- **No hiring risk.** You don't need to find, hire, and retain a raffle-specific operator — a role that doesn't exist at most charities and is genuinely hard to recruit.
- **No single-point-of-failure risk.** Your raffle doesn't collapse if your one in-house operator goes on vacation during the final week of a cycle.
- **Cross-client learnings.** A good agency is running 20+ raffles simultaneously and can spot patterns no single charity could.
- **Faster licensing.** Agencies have relationships with regulators and experience with what a clean application looks like.
- **Better platform economics.** Agencies negotiate platform rates across their portfolio and pass volume pricing through.

Here's a realistic year-over-year comparison for a mid-sized charity running a bi-annual 50/50 with ambitions of growing:

These aren't hypothetical numbers — they're in the range of what we see when we compare charities that went in-house to charities that hired experienced operators for similar programs.

The performance gap isn't about doing anything dramatically different. It's about forty small things being executed a little better every cycle, by someone whose entire job is executing them.

## When in-house actually does make sense

We'd be dishonest if we didn't acknowledge this. In-house is the right answer when:

1. **You have a genuinely dedicated, experienced raffle operator on staff** — someone who has run programs before, not a generalist development officer.
2. **Your program is small and stable.** If you're running a $40K parish picnic raffle every year and it works, keep doing it in-house.
3. **You have a multi-year runway to build the capability.** Some large health systems and university foundations have built impressive internal programs. It takes 3–5 years.
4. **You want full operational control** for strategic reasons specific to your organization.

If none of those conditions apply, the math on an agency partnership is almost always favorable.

## What to look for in an agency

If you decide to explore a management partner, the right ones will:

1. Show you *actual client results*, not vanity metrics.
2. Be transparent about what they handle vs. what stays with you.
3. Be AGCO- or state-registered where required, with documentation.
4. Offer a free exploratory call where they tell you honestly whether you're a fit.
5. Have a real team, not a single founder doing everything.

The wrong ones will:

1. Promise specific jackpot numbers without knowing your supporter base.
2. Refuse to share references or prior results.
3. Push you toward a specific platform regardless of fit.
4. Try to sign a contract on the first call.

## The bottom line

Running a raffle is a specialized operational discipline. Most charities don't have that capability in-house, and building it is expensive, slow, and risky. An experienced agency partner is often the cheaper path to better results — not because they're magic, but because they do this all day, every day, across many charities.

Do the math for your specific program. Get quotes from two or three agencies. Compare them honestly to the in-house numbers above. The answer usually becomes clear fairly quickly.
