The first 50/50 or Catch the Ace raffle your charity runs is almost never your best one. That's expected. But there's a difference between the normal learning curve of iteration — and seven specific, avoidable mistakes we see charities make over and over that can turn a promising first raffle into a cautionary tale.

If you're planning your first electronic raffle, read this. Twice.

## 1. Launching before licensing is fully in place

This is the single most expensive mistake, by a wide margin.

Charities — especially small ones — often assume that because they have a charitable registration, they're cleared to run a raffle. They are not. Every province (Canada) and nearly every state (U.S.) that permits raffles has its own licensing framework. In Ontario, raffles under $50,000 in total prizes are licensed municipally; above that, you need a provincial license from the AGCO.

**Why it's so expensive:** If you launch without the correct license, regulators have wide authority to halt the raffle, confiscate proceeds, and impose fines. More damagingly, your program's reputation with regulators becomes permanently harder — and your next application will face scrutiny.

**What to do instead:** Start the licensing conversation 3–4 months before your intended launch. Build the full prize structure, operating plan, and marketing materials *first*, then submit. Don't improvise mid-cycle.

## 2. Picking a platform before designing the program

Charities often shop for raffle platforms like they're shopping for SaaS: feature comparison, pricing, demo, done. This gets the problem exactly backwards.

The platform is a downstream decision. What you actually need to decide *first* is:

- What format (50/50, Catch the Ace, calendar raffle, progressive)?
- What cadence (weekly, monthly, continuous)?
- What geographic reach (provincial, national, cross-border)?
- What prize architecture (single grand, tiered, with early birds)?
- What marketing channels and cadence?
- What reporting and audit needs?

Only *after* that picture is clear can you rationally evaluate which platform fits. Charities who skip this step end up retrofitting their program to whatever the platform supports — and leave money on the table.

## 3. Underinvesting in the first-jackpot threshold

Here's the brutal truth about raffles: **nothing sells a raffle like a big jackpot**. And a big jackpot requires a big audience. And a big audience requires a marketing budget.

First-time raffle operators routinely try to run lean — minimal paid media, no PR agency, a small email list — and then wonder why the jackpot plateaued at $40,000.

The charities whose first raffles clear $500K+ typically invest $20K–$50K in first-cycle marketing: paid social, local radio, PR agency retainer, maybe a digital billboard or two. That sounds like a lot until you compare it to what you'd spend on a gala that nets the same.

**What to do instead:** Budget for marketing the way you'd budget for a product launch, not a charity campaign.

<Callout type="warning">
  A $0 marketing budget produces a $0 jackpot. A small jackpot produces a small press story, which produces a small next jackpot. The negative flywheel is real — and it's the single most common reason first raffles underperform.
</Callout>

## 4. Designing prize structures based on vibes

First-time operators tend to pick prize amounts based on what feels right. "Let's do a $50K early bird." Why $50K? "It sounds good."

It might sound good, but it probably isn't optimized. Prize architecture is a conversion lever. The right early-bird threshold pulls a meaningful share of your late-buyer traffic into the first week, smoothing your revenue curve and creating earlier press moments.

**What to do instead:** Design prize tiers against a revenue model. How much do you expect to raise in each week? What early-bird amount is meaningful to a ticket-buyer but small enough that you'll hit it early? What secondary milestones create natural press moments? This should be a spreadsheet, not a vibe.

## 5. Treating marketing as a launch-only activity

A raffle is not a product launch. It's a cadence.

Charities frequently invest heavily in pre-launch marketing and then go quiet — expecting the raffle to "sell itself" once it's live. The problem: ticket buyers need constant reminders. Not aggressive, annoying ones — but consistent, every-week touches that keep the raffle top-of-mind.

**What to do instead:** Build a *campaign* calendar that runs for the *full* duration of the raffle. Weekly email, rotating social creative, jackpot-milestone press releases, and paid media that scales up in the final weeks.

## 6. Outsourcing customer support to a bot

Running an active 50/50 is a live operation. Supporters have questions: "Did my ticket go through?" "When is the draw?" "Can I buy on behalf of my mom?"

Some of those questions come in at peak times — 7pm the night before the draw, when your raffle is doing $30K/hour. An unanswered question at that moment isn't a support issue; it's a revenue leak.

**What to do instead:** Have real humans available during peak hours. You don't need 24/7 staffing, but you do need someone on chat and email during the final 72 hours of the cycle.

## 7. Skipping the post-cycle retrospective

The difference between a charity whose second cycle is 20% bigger than their first — and one whose second cycle is flat — is almost always the presence or absence of a rigorous post-cycle review.

A good retrospective covers:
- Revenue-by-day, compared to forecast.
- Traffic source performance (what worked, what didn't).
- Email and SMS engagement metrics.
- Support ticket volume and common issues.
- Licensing and compliance notes.
- What we'd change next time.

Charities that skip this are doomed to repeat whatever suboptimal decisions they made in round one.

## The meta-mistake

If there's a single meta-mistake underlying all seven of these, it's this: **treating a raffle like a one-off fundraising event instead of an operational program.**

Events are designed, executed, and archived. Programs are launched, measured, iterated, and scaled. The charities that succeed at raffles adopt the program mindset from day one. The ones that don't find themselves running the same mediocre cycle over and over, wondering why the numbers aren't growing.

Your first raffle doesn't have to be perfect. It does have to teach you something — something specific enough to measurably improve the next one.
